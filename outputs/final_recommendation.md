## Final Recommendation

## Objective

The objective of this analysis was to identify the factors most strongly associated with monthly sales performance and provide evidence-based recommendations to support business decision-making.

---

## Key Findings

The regression analysis identified several variables that show a strong association with monthly sales.

### Most Important Drivers

#### Footfall

Footfall emerged as the strongest predictor of monthly sales across all models.

In the final multiple regression model, Footfall remained highly significant and showed a strong positive relationship with sales performance. Stores with higher customer traffic consistently generated higher sales.

#### Marketing Spend

Marketing Spend demonstrated a positive and statistically significant relationship with monthly sales.

Although its impact was smaller than Footfall, increased marketing investment was associated with improved sales outcomes.

#### Inventory Availability

Inventory Availability was also a significant positive predictor of sales.

Stores maintaining higher inventory availability tended to achieve higher sales, suggesting that stock availability is an important operational driver.

---

## Variables That Should Not Be Over-Interpreted

### Average Discount Percentage

Average Discount Percentage showed a negative coefficient but was not statistically significant at the 5% significance level.

As a result, leadership should avoid making major pricing or discounting decisions based solely on this model.

### Region

The Region_North dummy variable was not statistically significant.

After controlling for other variables, there was no meaningful evidence that stores in the North region performed differently from the reference region.

---

## Recommended Business Actions

Based on the regression results, leadership should prioritize:

1. Increasing customer footfall through targeted marketing and customer acquisition initiatives.
2. Continuing investment in marketing activities that effectively drive store traffic.
3. Maintaining strong inventory availability to reduce missed sales opportunities.
4. Monitoring operational practices in high-performing stores to identify best practices that can be replicated elsewhere.

---

## Risks and Limitations

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the results.

* The model explains approximately 80.7% of sales variation, but not all variation.
* Some business factors affecting sales may not be included in the dataset.
* Local market conditions, management quality, competition, and customer demographics may also influence performance.
* Residual analysis identified stores that performed substantially better or worse than predicted, suggesting that additional factors remain unmeasured.

---

## Association Does Not Prove Causation

Regression analysis identifies statistical associations between variables and sales performance.

However, regression alone does not prove that one variable directly causes changes in sales.

For example:

* Higher footfall is associated with higher sales, but regression cannot prove that increasing footfall alone will automatically increase sales.
* Other business factors may influence both footfall and sales simultaneously.

Therefore, leadership should use regression findings as decision-support evidence rather than proof of direct causal relationships.

---

## Final Recommendation

The Multiple Regression Model is recommended as the primary decision-support model because it provides the strongest explanatory power (R² = 0.8067) and incorporates multiple business drivers simultaneously.

The analysis suggests that leadership should focus primarily on:

* Increasing Footfall
* Optimizing Marketing Spend
* Maintaining High Inventory Availability

These variables show the strongest and most reliable association with monthly sales performance and provide the most actionable opportunities for business improvement.
